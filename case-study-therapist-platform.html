The Problem

Why most therapist websites lose patients

Open ten psychiatry websites in New York and eight of them start the same way. A photo of an office. A headline with a name, a degree and a city. A paragraph about years of experience and a list of approaches.

None of that is wrong. It is simply an answer to a question nobody asked. The person reading has spent weeks getting up the courage to search. They are not comparing credentials yet. They want to know if reaching out will feel safe, what happens next, and whether this person will understand them.

When the page does not answer that, they close the tab and open the next one. The practice never finds out. That is the cost, and it is invisible.

The Brief

A private psychiatry practice in New York, with no website at all

The patient in mind is an adult dealing with anxiety, mood shifts or long stretches of feeling overloaded. Someone who has decided to do something about it but has not contacted anyone yet.

That single detail shaped everything. This is not a returning patient looking up an address. This is a first step. The site has one job: make sending that first message feel easy.

The question behind every decision: what does this person feel in the ninety seconds before they either close the tab or press contact?
The Work

Design decisions, explained

Five choices that changed how the page performs. Each one starts with what was done, then why, then what the practice gains from it.

01 · The colours

Warm neutrals, terra cotta and sage on an off white base. No clinical blues.

Cool blue and white is the colour of a waiting room. It is what the person is trying to work up the courage to walk into. Warm tones read as someone's room rather than a facility, and that reads before a single word does.

The page feels approachable in the first half second, which is exactly when a nervous visitor decides whether to keep reading.

02 · The headline

"Feel steady again. A plan you can trust." Written about the patient, not the practice.

Nobody searches thinking "I need a board certified MD". They search thinking "I want to feel better". The headline says back to them what they already feel, so they recognise themselves on the page instead of reading a resume.

The visitor understands what this practice is for in under three seconds, and feels understood at the same time.

Avoided "Welcome to the practice of Dr. Hart, MD, Psychiatrist in New York"
Used "Feel steady again. A plan you can trust."
03 · Three words under the buttons

Confidential. No pressure. Clear steps.

The three things that stop someone from reaching out are almost always the same: who will find out, will I be pushed into something, and what happens after I press send. Naming all three answers them before the visitor has to ask, and asking is the part they were dreading.

The most common reasons for hesitating are removed at the exact moment of hesitation.

04 · What the button says

"Contact me", not "Book Now" or "Schedule Appointment".

Booking sounds like a decision. A message sounds like a question. Most people who land on the page are still deciding, and asking them to commit at that moment loses them. The second button, "How I help", gives the ones who want to read more somewhere to go instead of leaving.

More first contacts from the largest group on the page, the people who are still thinking about it.

05 · How the services are written

Each service describes the visit, not the procedure.

"A comprehensive assessment of your mental health history" describes a process. "We take the time to understand the whole picture, and you leave with a clearer explanation" describes an hour of someone's life. The second one lets the reader picture being there.

The relationship starts before the first session is booked, which is what turns a visitor into a patient.

Under the hood

Speed, and why it decides everything else

None of the choices above matter if the page is still loading. On a phone, on mobile data, three seconds is the difference between a new patient and a back button. Every site is built mobile first, in plain HTML and CSS, with nothing loaded from anywhere else.

100 PageSpeed target
0 External dependencies
HTML/CSS Vanilla stack
Mobile First, always

Nothing on the page jumps or shifts while it loads. The text is readable at every screen width, headings follow a logical order for screen readers, and every image has a description. The FAQ opens and closes with no JavaScript at all, so it works even if a script fails.

On the scroll animations: everything is visible the moment the page loads. The fade in is a bonus, never a condition. If JavaScript is slow or blocked, nothing is hidden.
Common questions

What practices usually ask before starting

How much does a therapist website cost?

Projects start at $1,500. That covers strategy, copy, design and code. You are paying for a site built to bring in new patients, not a template with your name on it.

How long does it take to build?

Two to three weeks for a practice site of this size, once the photos and practice details are ready. Most of the time goes into the writing and the structure, not the code.

Do I need a blog to show up on Google?

No. For a local practice, separate pages for each service plus a clear FAQ do far more than a blog nobody has time to update. Google reads those pages as the answer to what people in your city are searching for.

What about HIPAA and the contact form?

The form asks for a name, an email and a short message, and never for clinical detail. Anything beyond a first hello is pointed to your secure intake system, so the website itself never holds sensitive information.

Can you write the copy as well?

Yes, and it is not an add on. The words and the layout are the same decision. Writing is included in every project.

Do you work with practices outside New York?

Yes. The work is done remotely and the approach is the same in any city. Only the local search wording changes.

The takeaway

The patient's experience starts long before the first session

Every choice on this site was checked against one question: does this make it easier or harder for a nervous person to take the first step? Colour, wording, layout, the text on a button. All of it answers to that.

That is the difference between a website that looks professional and one that brings in patients. The goal was never to impress the psychiatrist. It was to reassure the person reading at eleven at night, deciding whether to send a message.

This is a demonstration project. The practice and the practitioner are illustrative, the design and code are real and live.
