HIPAA and Your Therapy Website: What You Actually Need to Know

Note: This article provides general information regarding HIPAA and therapist websites. It does not constitute legal advice. For specific compliance questions, consult a healthcare attorney.

Every US therapist thinking about building a website faces the exact same question at some point: "Does my site need to be HIPAA compliant?" Anxiety, confusion, and often terrible advice accompany this question.

The reality remains simpler than it appears. Understanding it helps you make the right decisions for your site without unnecessary stress.

In this article, we explain exactly what HIPAA dictates for websites, which elements of your site it affects, and what you actually need to watch out for in practice.

What HIPAA Is and What It Covers

HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act) is US legislation protecting patient medical records. It applies to what we call Protected Health Information (PHI)—any information that can identify a patient combined with their health data.

If you are a psychologist, therapist, or mental health professional accepting insurance or working with clearinghouses, you fall under HIPAA rules. This doesn't just cover your filing cabinets. It covers every aspect of your business operations, including your website.

Where HIPAA Actually Affects Your Website

Here lies the crucial point most people misunderstand: HIPAA does not apply to your website as a whole. It applies strictly to specific touchpoints where patient information processing happens.

Let's break it down:

The Home Page, "About" Page, and Services Pages

These pages involve zero patient information. No one transmits medical data by reading your homepage. These pages carry absolutely no HIPAA compliance requirements regarding their design or content.

The Contact Form

Here is where the rules kick in. If your contact form only asks for a name, an email, and a generic message (e.g., "I would like to schedule an appointment"), this alone does not constitute Protected Health Information.

If the form asks for diagnostic details, symptoms, or other medical specifics, you enter territory requiring extreme care. The best practice? Keep your contact form ruthlessly simple. Ask only for the basic details needed to initiate a conversation.

The Email You Receive from the Form

This is highly sensitive. If a patient emails you medical information, you must protect that email. This means you need an email provider who has signed a Business Associate Agreement (BAA) with you and explicitly supports HIPAA compliance.

Standard Gmail fails this test. Specific Google Workspace editions and other dedicated providers support HIPAA. This requirement dictates what happens after the website interaction, not your website's design.

Online Scheduling (If Present)

If your site features an online booking system collecting patient data, that system must be HIPAA compliant. Platforms like SimplePractice, TherapyNotes, and others are built specifically for mental health professionals and already handle HIPAA compliance.

If you use these tools and simply embed them into your site, you drop the HIPAA worry entirely. The platform handles the heavy lifting.

Google Analytics and Other Tracking Tools

Many overlook this touchpoint. Google Analytics collects visitor data. If a visitor is also a patient, and you can use that data to identify them and link them to a therapy visit, you face a HIPAA question.

In practice, Google Analytics alone does not store medical data. But if you run highly specific remarketing campaigns, you cross into problematic territory. The golden rule: use analytics to improve your site's performance, but entirely avoid remarketing campaigns targeting your site visitors as potential patients.

HIPAA does not forbid you from having a website. It simply demands vigilance regarding how you handle the information you collect.

Three Things You Must Do Immediately

1. Ensure your site uses an SSL certificate

This is the absolute baseline. An HTTPS site (rather than HTTP) encrypts the data transferred between the visitor's browser and your server. Every modern site requires this. If your site displays "Not Secure" in the address bar, fix it immediately.

2. Keep the contact form simple

Never ask for sensitive medical information on your website's contact form. Name, email, phone number, and a brief message suffice. Discuss the clinical details later through secure channels.

3. Use HIPAA-compliant tools for what matters

For emails containing patient data, use a provider who signed a BAA. For online scheduling, use a platform built specifically for healthcare. For telehealth, use a HIPAA-compliant video platform.

Which Tools Are Actually HIPAA Compliant

Here is a breakdown of frequently used tool categories and their general standing. Always verify independently and demand a BAA where required.

For online booking and patient records

Platforms like SimplePractice, TherapyNotes, and TheraNest are built exclusively for therapists and fully support HIPAA compliance. You simply integrate them into your site as external booking systems.

For email

Google Workspace (with a BAA), Hushmail for Healthcare, ProtonMail for Business, and other providers support HIPAA. The key is securing the signed BAA, not just using a "secure" platform.

For telehealth

Regular Zoom is not HIPAA compliant out of the box. You need Zoom for Healthcare with a BAA, Doxy.me, VSee, or similar platforms engineered for healthcare environments.

What You DO NOT Need to Worry About

Massive misinformation surrounds HIPAA and websites. Let’s clear up what isn't a problem.

Your site’s design does not concern HIPAA. Colors, typography, photos, layouts. None of these relate to HIPAA compliance.

Your site’s content does not concern HIPAA. Writing articles about anxiety, depression, or any condition has zero HIPAA implications. You are providing general information, not processing patient records.

A basic contact form is not automatically a problem. If you ask only for basic contact details to start a conversation, you do not enter HIPAA territory.

Website hosting alone is not an issue, provided you do not store medical data on the server itself.

The Practical Rule: Separate the "Marketing" from the "Clinical" Experience

The simplest way to frame this: your website operates as a marketing tool. Its sole purpose is to present who you are, what you do, and to initiate contact.

The moment someone becomes a patient, the clinical relationship begins. That is where you deploy your HIPAA-compliant tools: secure email, electronic health records (EHR), and secure video for telehealth.

Keep that boundary ruthlessly clean, and HIPAA becomes entirely manageable.

Your website is the storefront. Your HIPAA-compliant applications are the clinic. Keep them separate, and you avoid the traps.

What a "HIPAA Compliant Website" Actually Means in Practice

When a web designer claims they build "HIPAA compliant websites," ask them exactly what they mean. The website itself does not get "audited" for HIPAA compliance. What gets audited is how you handle patient data.

You can design a website to radically minimize risk: enforce SSL, use a bare-bones contact form, integrate with dedicated HIPAA-compliant booking platforms, and eliminate remarketing trackers. This constitutes excellent practice. But the website itself holds no magical HIPAA "certification."

The Questions You Must Ask Your Web Designer

When discussing your site with a web designer, ask these three questions directly:

First, does the site use SSL? (The answer must be an immediate yes.)

Second, does the contact form send data to a secure server? What happens to that data?

Third, if you want online booking, is the recommended platform explicitly built for healthcare?

If the designer answers these with absolute clarity, you are in safe hands. If they stumble, it signals a dangerous lack of experience building sites for healthcare professionals.

The Real Reason HIPAA Shouldn't Hold You Back

Many therapists use HIPAA anxieties as an excuse to delay building their website. "I don't know if it will be compliant, so I won't do it." This is a trap.

Lacking a website does not protect you from HIPAA. It simply renders you invisible to prospective patients. And HIPAA, as we broke down, targets very specific touchpoints on your site, not the site itself.

Make the right decisions at the right touchpoints, and you build a website that dominates as a marketing tool while perfectly aligning with HIPAA requirements.

The Bottom Line: What to Remember

HIPAA does not care about your site's design or content. It cares about how you handle patient information. A secure site (HTTPS), a streamlined contact form, and dedicated HIPAA-compliant tools for booking and communication cover your baseline needs.

For specific legal questions, always consult a specialized healthcare attorney. But do not let these fears stop you from building a digital presence that accurately represents you and brings the right patients through your door.
